Seasonal Allergies:
Symptoms and Solutions

Most people look forward to the change of seasons. The days get longer, the leaves change colours , flowers bloom, and the weather gets warmer. As nice as it sounds in theory, many people suffer during the transition into these warmer months. Seasonal allergies are the source of many a runny nose or itchy eyes, but exactly what are allergies?

According to the Asthma Canada Foundation, allergies occur or flare up when allergens, usually pollen or other environmental substances, enter your body causing an immune system response. An allergy that occurs more in a specific season is commonly referred to as “seasonal allergies” or “hay fever.” The term hay fever originated from hay cutting season, traditionally in the summer months. As you can imagine, this hay cutting resulted in more airborne allergens and an influx of allergy symptoms. While spring and summer are certainly the peak, it’s possible to suffer from seasonal allergies year-round. Let’s roll up our sleeves and dig in to the symptoms, causes, and treatments for seasonal allergies.

Symptoms of Seasonal Allergies

Seasonal allergies can present themselves in many different ways. Probably the first thing people think of and associate with seasonal allergies is sneezing. It makes sense, your body is attempting to rid itself of the allergens irritating your airways. It’s common for people with asthma to have asthma attacks triggered by seasonal allergens if they have seasonal allergies as well. The most common symptoms are:

  • Sneezing
  • Runny nose
  • Nasal congestion
  • Itchy, irrigated eyes
  • Itchy throat and ears
  • Clogged ears
  • Headache

While the symptoms may present themselves in many different ways depending on the person, the causes of seasonal allergies are pretty easy to pinpoint.

Causes of Seasonal Allergies

As we mentioned in the beginning of this article, though seasonal allergies are most commonly associated with the spring and summer months, they can affect people year-round. Let’s take a look at the different causes of seasonal allergies throughout the year.

  • Spring : The most common culprit causing seasonal allergies are trees! Though the flowering blossoms may be beautiful to look at in the spring, chances are they are the root of your pain. Tree pollen is a well-known allergen and usually peaks in late April or early May. Next time you stop to appreciate the flowers, maybe think twice!
  • Summer : Summer is the season during which the name hay fever originated, and the most common allergen is, once again, pollen. The source of this pollen is different from the spring months though, and it’s usually grass pollen that triggers people’s seasonal allergies. The peak for this kind of allergen runs from late May to about mid-July.
  • Fall : As the days get colder in the autumn, many of the sources of allergens start to die, resulting in fewer instances of hay fever. However, one thing that can still trigger seasonal allergies during this season is ragweed. Ragweed only lives for one season, but during this time, ragweed pollen is still potent enough to cause an unwanted bout of sneezing. Ragweed season usually lasts from mid to late August, and peters out in October.
  • Winter : By the time winter rolls around, most outdoor plants, and their resulting allergens are dormant. However, during the winter, due to increased hours spent indoors, those prone to seasonal allergies may find themselves suffering from indoor allergies. Things such as mold, dust, and pet dander are common indoor allergens that may trigger symptoms even in the winter.
